A linear probe reads refusal from your model's activations with 98% accuracy. You steer along that direction — behaviour shifts. You ship the finding: *the model encodes refusal in this layer.* Three weeks later the steering vector miscalibrates out of distribution, the probe fails under a paraphrase, and an ablation that suppressed the behaviour on your test set does nothing on the deployment traffic.

This isn't bad luck. It's a **category error about evidence**, and it has a hundred-year-old diagnosis.

## Every interpretability question sits on a rung

Causal inference sorts questions into Pearl's three-rung hierarchy: **associational** (what correlates with what), **interventional** (what happens if I manipulate this), and **counterfactual** (what *would have* happened, for this very input, under an unobserved change). Different rungs demand different evidence, and evidence from a lower rung cannot license claims from a higher one — no matter how much of it you collect.

Interpretability methods sit on this ladder whether they acknowledge it or not: probing is associational; ablation, patching, and steering are interventional; "the feature *means* X" and "*the* circuit computing Y" are counterfactual-strength claims about uniqueness and semantics.

The second question is the one the field skips: even at the right rung, **how many answers are consistent with your evidence?** Try both questions below.

The cloud is the point: evidence doesn't hand you *the* answer, it rules answers out. Climbing rungs shrinks the set. What remains at the top is still an **equivalence class** — and whether it collapses to a single answer *up to harmless symmetries* is exactly the question of **identifiability**.

## One claim, two gaps

<div id="animations" />

Take the claim from the opening story — *"the model encodes refusal in this layer."* It fails in two independent ways, and they compound.

<video controls loop muted playsInline className="rounded-lg border border-zinc-200 dark:border-zinc-800 w-full my-4" src="/causality/animation-2.mp4" />

**Gap 1 — the rung gap.** The claim, as used downstream (steer it! edit it!), requires interventional evidence: *the model uses this direction in its computation.* The method provides associational evidence: *a probe fires on refusal prompts.* Decodability does not imply model use — the probe may exploit token length, position artefacts, or any confounder that co-varies with refusal.

**Gap 2 — the identification gap.** The claim says **the** direction, as if the data singled one out. But multiple probes and SAE features decode refusal equally well. The evidence defines an equivalence class of solutions; the method returned *one member* of it, chosen by initialisation and optimiser quirks.

## We audited 186 claims

The patterns are systematic, not sloppy: patching results (L2) narrated with counterfactual verbs ("encodes", "THE circuit"); probing findings (L1) with interventional ones; single-distribution results generalised past their scope. This reflects missing terminological infrastructure, not unreliable science — which is exactly why a shared causal vocabulary helps.

## Why low loss can't identify anything

Here is the part that is a theorem rather than a taxonomy. A method learns a representation $\mathbf{h} = \phi(\mathbf{a})$ from activations $\mathbf{a}$, and the claim is that $\mathbf{h}$ recovers concepts $\mathbf{c}$. But for any invertible map $v$, the pair $(\phi' , \psi') = (v \circ \phi, \psi \circ v^{-1})$ achieves exactly the same reconstruction loss — the objective cannot distinguish a solution from its remixes. Without additional structure, unsupervised recovery of latents is impossible (Hyvärinen & Pajunen, 1999; Locatello et al., 2019). Reconstruction plus sparsity is a useful inductive bias, not an identification guarantee.

**Causal representation learning** (CRL) supplies the missing structure: interventions, paired contexts, sparsity of *changes*. Under such conditions the recovered representation is unique up to permutation and scaling,

$$
\hat{\mathbf{c}} = \mathbf{P}\boldsymbol{\Lambda}\mathbf{c},
$$

where $\mathbf{P}$ is a permutation and $\boldsymbol{\Lambda}$ a diagonal scaling — the latent is pinned down *as a set of axes*, with only harmless relabelling left over. Sparse shift autoencoders (SSAEs) realise this recipe on LLM activations: paired samples over uniformly sampled contexts identify the varied latents (Joshi et al., 2025).

**Identifiability is interaction-relative.** Two iron filings — one magnetised, one not — are indistinguishable under looking, weighing, lifting. Bring a magnet and the difference is suddenly visible. What a method can identify is bounded by the interactions it affords; probing, patching, and steering are different magnets, and a representation may show structure only under the right one.

## The research programme

The failure modes above are addressable, and each direction pairs a CRL tool with an interpretability testbed: **counterfactual safety** (when does activation patching coincide with a true counterfactual — and what minimal annotation restores the correspondence where it doesn't?); **task-relative identification** (steering may need only blockwise identifiability, editing needs elementwise — demand no more than the task requires); **compositional control** (task vectors compose, model edits don't — when do interventions compose, and does stronger identification predict it?); and **transportability** (edit failures under shift are transportability violations — predict which prompts an edit survives *before* deploying it).
